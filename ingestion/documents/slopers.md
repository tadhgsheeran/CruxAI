# Slopers

## What a sloper is

Slopers are rounded, friction-dependent holds without positive edges, which must be gripped with an open hand across as much surface area as possible. Rather than pulling down on an edge, the climber pulls back against the hold, using friction between the skin and the hold surface to prevent the hand from sliding off. Sloper strength depends on shoulder engagement, hand position, body position under the hold, and the friction available from skin condition and temperature. They are among the most technique-dependent hold types in climbing.

## When it is useful

Slopers matter on granite features (particularly balls and rounded arêtes), resin blobs and volumes in gyms, roofs where large sloping features dominate, and modern competition setting. Sport routes and boulders on limestone tufas often involve sloping pinches and shoulders. Climbers who train only on edges and jugs will find themselves powerless on sloper-heavy setting despite strong fingers, since sloper strength is a distinct capacity built on shoulders, contact area, and body position rather than pure finger force.

## Common mistakes

Common mistakes include gripping the edges of the hold as if they were positive, when there are no edges to grip, and failing to maximize skin contact by keeping the palm off the hold. Letting the hips drop away from the wall increases the outward force on the hand and causes slopers to slip. Cold hands and sweaty skin both destroy sloper friction, but climbers often ignore temperature and skin management. Pulling with a bent arm rather than a straight arm reduces the effective downward force and shifts load onto the fingers.

## Training and practice

Effective training includes open-hand hangs on sloper rungs of a hangboard, focusing on shoulder engagement and full hand contact. Board climbing with sloper-only problems on the Kilter or a spray wall trains the specific coordination of body position and grip. Wide pinch training on portable blocks transfers reasonably well. Deliberate practice on sloper-heavy boulders, focusing on hip position and shoulder engagement rather than grip force, builds the technique component that is often more limiting than raw strength. Skin management (sanding, moisturizing on rest days) directly improves sloper performance.

## Safety

Slopers loaded with straight arms place significant demand on the shoulder, and repeated hard sloper sessions can aggravate the rotator cuff and biceps tendon. Include pressing work and shoulder stability exercises to balance the pulling load. Chalk management matters more on slopers than any other hold type, since even slight moisture destroys friction, so brush holds thoroughly and manage skin hydration on session days.
