# Overhang Technique

## What overhang technique is

Overhang technique refers to the body positioning, tension, and movement patterns required to climb walls that lean past vertical. Because gravity pulls the climber away from the wall rather than into it, the arms must actively hold the body in and the core must resist the swing. Key elements include keeping hips close to the wall, engaging toe and heel hooks to lock the lower body in place, and using straight-arm hanging positions whenever possible to preserve grip strength. Movement tends to be more dynamic and momentum-driven than on vertical terrain.

## When it is useful

Overhang technique matters on cave problems, roofs, most modern gym setting, and the majority of hard sport routes at the elite level. It is also increasingly present in competition, where volumes and overhanging panels dominate. Climbers who cannot manage steep terrain will hit a ceiling in the mid to upper grades regardless of finger strength, since the ability to keep feet on the wall and generate movement from the core is the actual limiting factor once the wall angle exceeds about fifteen degrees past vertical.

## Common mistakes

Common mistakes include letting the hips sag away from the wall, which loads the arms unnecessarily and cuts feet prematurely. Climbers over-grip because they feel insecure, burning finger endurance early. Others fail to use straight-arm positions and instead climb every move locked off, doubling the metabolic cost. Missed toe hooks and heel hooks, particularly on volumes and prow features, force pure upper-body pulling when leverage was available. Rushing through moves rather than settling weight before each pull also wastes energy.

## Training and practice

Front lever progressions (tuck, advanced tuck, straddle, full) build the core and pulling strength needed for steep terrain. Tension boards like the Kilter, Moonboard, and Tension Board train steep climbing under load. Feet-on and feet-cut drills, where you deliberately alternate between keeping feet on the wall and swinging them cut, teach both tension and recovery. Straight-arm hang practice on jugs, focusing on hanging from bone and connective tissue rather than muscle, trains the efficient body position that separates good steep climbers from stronger but less efficient ones.

## Safety

Steep climbing loads shoulders heavily, particularly during cut-foot swings and campus-style moves. Rotator cuff and biceps tendon issues are common in climbers who train steep terrain without complementary pressing and stability work. Avoid campusing when fatigued, and include face pulls, external rotations, and pressing movements to keep the shoulder healthy under repeated high-load pulling.
