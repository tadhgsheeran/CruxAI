# Power Endurance

## What power endurance is

Power endurance is the ability to sustain near-maximal effort for durations of roughly 30 seconds to 3 minutes, spanning the gap between pure power (bouldering-length efforts) and aerobic endurance (long routes at moderate intensity). It is the energy system that governs performance on hard boulder circuits, sustained crux sections on sport routes, and any climbing where you must execute powerful moves while already pumped. Power endurance is trained by working at intensities high enough to accumulate lactate but sustained long enough to develop the capacity to buffer and clear it.

## When it is useful

Power endurance matters on sport routes in the 8a and above range where cruxes are long, on competition boulders with sustained hard sections, and on any route where the crux comes after significant climbing. It is the trait that separates climbers who can pull the moves fresh from climbers who can actually link them under fatigue. For redpointing, it is often the specific fitness that unlocks a project after the individual moves are dialed. Boulderers who compete or attempt long, sustained problems also depend on power endurance heavily.

## Common mistakes

Common mistakes include training power endurance at intensities that are too easy, which develops aerobic capacity but not the target quality. Others rest too long between sets, which turns the workout into repeated power work rather than power endurance. Mixing power endurance with maximal strength or bouldering in the same session compromises both, since fatigued fingers cannot express true max strength. Doing power endurance too frequently (more than twice per week) leads to accumulated fatigue and stalled progress.

## Training and practice

Classic protocols include 4x4s (four boulder problems climbed back to back four times with rest between sets), linked boulders (chaining two or three problems into a longer sequence), and on-the-minute burns where you start a hard route every minute until failure. Route intervals (climb, rest a fixed time proportional to climbing time, repeat) train the specific work-to-rest ratio of sport climbing. Sessions should feel genuinely pumpy, and the last set should be close to failure. Two focused sessions per week is a typical prescription during a power endurance block.

## Safety

Power endurance training loads the fingers and elbows heavily under fatigue, which is when injuries most often occur. Warm up thoroughly before starting the intensive sets, and stop when technique breaks down significantly rather than pushing to complete failure. Antagonist work and adequate rest days between power endurance sessions are essential to avoid tendinopathy from accumulated stress.
