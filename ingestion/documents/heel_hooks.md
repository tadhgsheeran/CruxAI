# Heel Hooks

## What a heel hook is

A heel hook is a foot technique where you place your heel on a hold and pull with your hamstrings and glutes, effectively turning your leg into a third pulling contact point rather than a pushing point. Unlike standard footwork that drives the body upward, heel hooks pull the body toward or around a hold, redistributing load from the fingers and arms into the posterior chain. They can be applied to jugs, edges, volumes, arêtes, and even sloped features when the geometry allows a solid catch behind the heel bone. The pulling action is generated by contracting the hamstring and rotating the hip, not by squeezing the toes.

## When it is useful

Heel hooks matter most on steep terrain, roofs, and arêtes where staying attached to the wall would otherwise require full body tension from the upper body alone. They also unlock mantle moves, help you rock over onto slabs, and create critical rest positions on overhanging routes by allowing you to shake out one hand. On modern competition boulders with large volumes, a well-placed heel is often the difference between sending and falling. Sport climbers use them to preserve finger strength through crux sections, and boulderers use them to generate leverage on committing throws.

## Common mistakes

Common mistakes include placing the heel without actively weighting it, using the instep or side of the foot instead of the true heel, and letting the hip drop away from the wall so the pull line collapses. Climbers also forget to look at the heel while placing it, which results in sloppy contact, and they under-engage the hamstring by leaving the leg passive rather than actively pulling. Over-camming, where you rotate the toe inward too aggressively, can strain the knee and reduce the effective pull angle.

## Training and practice

Good drills include the hover drill, where you place a heel and hold the position without moving for five to ten seconds to build engagement awareness. Sustained heel-hook circuits on a spray wall or moonboard train force production and precision under fatigue. Deliberate repeats, where you climb a problem and then reclimb focusing only on heel placement quality, are more effective than volume alone. Isolated hamstring work such as Nordic curls and single-leg Romanian deadlifts builds the pulling capacity that translates directly to the wall.

## Safety

Heel hooks carry real hamstring and knee injury risk, especially when placed high above the head and loaded suddenly with a full-body pull. Warm up hips, hamstrings, and adductors before hard sessions, and avoid aggressive high heels when muscles are cold or already fatigued from a long session.
