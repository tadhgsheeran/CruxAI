# Deadpoint Technique

A deadpoint is a controlled dynamic climbing movement in which the climber reaches for the next hold near the moment when upward momentum slows.

The technique can help a climber reach holds that are difficult to access statically without requiring a fully explosive jump.

A successful deadpoint usually involves coordinated leg drive, hip movement, accurate timing, and a relaxed reaching arm.

Practice should begin on comfortable holds and predictable moves. The goal is to improve timing and accuracy rather than simply pulling harder with the arms.

## Overshooting Dynamic Holds

Overshooting a hold during a deadpoint usually means the climber generated too much momentum or reached after the body had already passed the moment of weightlessness.

A deadpoint should be timed so the hand contacts the hold near the top of the body's upward movement, when vertical momentum briefly slows. If the climber pulls too hard, jumps too far, or releases the feet too early, the body can continue past the target.

Practice using smaller amounts of force and aiming to arrive at the hold rather than launching through it. Controlled deadpoint drills on comfortable holds can help develop timing and accuracy.
