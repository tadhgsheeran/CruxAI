# Crimping

## What crimping is

Crimping is a finger grip position where the fingers are bent sharply at the second knuckle (PIP joint) to load a small edge, with the thumb either resting alongside (half crimp) or wrapped over the index finger (full crimp). It is the highest-force grip position climbers use and generates significantly more finger strength on small edges than open-hand grips, but it also places the highest strain on the finger pulleys and flexor tendons. The half crimp is a training staple and a general-purpose grip; the full crimp is reserved for the smallest holds where nothing else works.

## When it is useful

Crimping matters on granite edges, limestone crimp rails, thin faces, and any hold too small or too positive-shaped to grip open-handed. On competition setting, small screw-on edges often demand crimps. Board climbing on the Kilter, Moonboard, and Tension Board relies heavily on crimping strength, and elite outdoor climbs above 8a routinely include crimp cruxes. Crimp strength also transfers well to other grip types, making it a foundational training focus for most improving climbers past the intermediate level.

## Common mistakes

Common mistakes include full crimping when a half crimp would suffice, which unnecessarily loads the pulleys. Failing to engage the thumb reduces the effective grip strength, and over-gripping (using more force than the move requires) burns finger endurance quickly. Many climbers crimp with a hyperextended wrist, which reduces force transfer and stresses the wrist. Progressing hangboard loads too aggressively, particularly on small edges, is the classic path to a pulley injury.

## Training and practice

Structured hangboard protocols are the primary training method. Max hangs (10-second holds at high load, three to five minutes rest) build maximum strength, while repeaters (7 seconds on, 3 seconds off, six or seven cycles) build strength endurance. No-hang devices allow finger-specific loading without shoulder involvement and are easier to progress precisely. Small-edge protocols (typically 20mm and 15mm edges) target the specific position most relevant to hard climbing. Progression should be slow, with load increases of no more than 5 to 10 percent per week and mandatory deload weeks.

## Safety

Full crimping is the highest-risk finger position for A2 and A4 pulley injuries, particularly on dynamic loading or when fatigued. Warm up finger flexors thoroughly with rice bucket work, band exercises, or easy climbing before hard crimping. Avoid full crimping when fatigued or when a hold can be gripped half-crimped, and never full crimp on a dynamic catch if you can avoid it. Any sudden pop or sharp pain in a finger during a crimp should end the session immediately.
