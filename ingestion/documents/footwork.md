# Footwork

## What footwork is

Footwork refers to the precise placement, weighting, and management of the feet on holds throughout a climb. Good footwork means selecting the right part of the shoe (inside edge, outside edge, or toe), placing it silently on the exact intended point of the hold, and trusting the weight through it without adjustment. Because the legs are stronger than the arms and the feet carry most of the load on anything less than steeply overhanging terrain, footwork determines how efficiently a climber moves and how long they can sustain effort before the arms fail.

## When it is useful

Footwork matters on every style of climb but becomes decisive on slabs, technical vertical faces, and long endurance routes where the arms cannot compensate for poor feet. On slab, weight distribution and edge angle are the entire climb. On overhangs, precise feet enable body tension, keep the hips close to the wall, and prevent cutting feet unnecessarily. On sport routes, good footwork is what allows shaking out at semi-rests and executing efficient sequences at the redpoint stage.

## Common mistakes

Common mistakes include readjusting the foot after initial placement, which wastes energy and signals imprecision, and using the instep instead of a defined edge, which reduces control. Climbers often look away from the foot before it lands, resulting in inaccurate contact, and let feet land noisily, which indicates uncontrolled weighting. Cutting feet unnecessarily on steep terrain also wastes energy, as does failing to swap feet when the sequence requires it.

## Training and practice

The classic silent feet drill (climb an easy problem trying to make no sound with the feet) builds precision and control. Glue feet, where you place a foot and refuse to move it until you leave the hold entirely, trains commitment. No-hands slab climbing forces awareness of weight distribution and edge angle. Foot swap practice on progressively smaller holds develops a skill most intermediate climbers neglect. Climbing routes several grades below your limit while focusing exclusively on foot precision is one of the highest-return uses of gym time.

## Safety

Footwork does not carry direct injury risk in the way finger or shoulder work does, but shoe fit and condition matter. Overly worn rubber on the edge reduces reliability, and shoes that are too loose transmit force poorly. Ankles can be sprained if a foot pops from a small edge onto a bad landing, so bouldering awareness and spotting matter more when feet are unreliable.
