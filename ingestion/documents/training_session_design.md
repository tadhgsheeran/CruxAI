# Training Session Design

## What training session design is

Training session design is the structuring of a climbing or supplementary training session so that each element (warmup, main work, supplementary work, and cooldown) supports the intended adaptation without interfering with it. A well-designed session prioritizes the highest-quality work when the nervous system is fresh, sequences training modalities to avoid contamination between energy systems, and manages total load so recovery is possible before the next session. Poor session design is one of the most common reasons intermediate climbers stop progressing despite training hard.

## When it matters

Session design matters for every deliberate training day, including climbing sessions with a specific goal, hangboard workouts, supplementary strength work, and endurance sessions. It matters most during focused training blocks (strength, power, power endurance, or endurance phases), where the goal is a specific adaptation rather than general climbing volume. Climbers who show up to the gym and just climb are getting maintenance at best; climbers who design sessions with a clear stimulus and appropriate load are the ones who progress predictably.

## Common mistakes

Common mistakes include inadequate warmups (jumping straight to hard climbing or hangboarding), mixing incompatible modalities in the same session (max strength work after endurance climbing when fingers are already fatigued), and going to failure on every set rather than leaving one or two reps in reserve. Others fail to progress load session over session, doing the same workout for months, or stack too many high-intensity sessions back to back without recovery days between. Skipping cooldowns and antagonist work is a slower but reliable path to injury.

## Training and practice

A general session template runs warmup (10 to 20 minutes of pulse-raising and joint mobility, progressing to easy climbing), main work (the highest-quality work, targeting the session's primary adaptation), supplementary work (volume or accessory training that supports but does not overlap with the main work), and cooldown (easy movement and antagonist exercises). Sequence hardest first: max strength before power, power before power endurance, and endurance work in its own session where possible. Reverse pyramid loading (heaviest sets first) and block periodization (weeks focused on a single quality) are proven approaches for structuring both individual sessions and training cycles.

## Safety

Monitor session RPE (rate of perceived exertion) at the end of each session, and avoid stacking two RPE 9 or 10 sessions back to back without a rest day. Track finger and elbow soreness in the 24 to 48 hours after sessions, and reduce load if pain persists. Deload weeks every four to six weeks are non-negotiable for anyone training seriously; they are the mechanism by which the adaptations you have been driving actually consolidate.
