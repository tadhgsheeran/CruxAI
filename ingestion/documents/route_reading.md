# Route Reading

## What route reading is

Route reading is the process of visually identifying the sequence of holds, foot positions, rest stances, and cruxes on a climb before physically attempting it. Done from the ground or from a rest position mid-route, it involves matching your body dimensions and style to the geometry of the wall to predict how each move will unfold. A good read anticipates which hand goes where, which foot follows, where you can shake out, and where the hardest moves are located so you can budget effort accordingly.

## When it is useful

Route reading matters most on onsight and flash attempts, where you have no prior knowledge of the sequence and must solve the puzzle in real time before committing. It also matters when redpointing, since a memorized sequence tuned to your body is faster and more efficient than improvising. In competition climbing, isolation-period reading is often the difference between topping and falling. On multi-pitch and outdoor sport climbing, reading rest positions and pump-management sections is essential for finishing the route with reserves.

## Common mistakes

Common mistakes include reading hands only and neglecting feet, which is where most climbers actually fail on unfamiliar routes. Others miss rest stances, meaning they climb through positions where they could have recovered, or they fail to plan breathing and chalking points. Reading too quickly and committing to the first plausible sequence rather than considering alternatives is another frequent error, as is projecting your preferred style onto a route that actually demands something different (for example, reading a route as static when it wants momentum).

## Training and practice

Effective drills include reading routes aloud with a partner and comparing interpretations before climbing, which forces you to articulate the sequence explicitly. Video review of your attempts against your pre-climb read shows where predictions broke down. Forced sequence prediction, where you commit to a full read and refuse to deviate mid-climb, builds commitment and reveals reading errors clearly. In the gym, reading five routes at a grade above your onsight level and then climbing them tests and calibrates your reading accuracy over time.

## Safety

Route reading itself carries no injury risk, but poor reading can lead to fatigue-driven falls in bad positions. Reading clipping stances is particularly important on sport routes, since clipping from a poor position increases fall distance and can result in clip-related injuries. Always identify the clip points during your read, not mid-crux.
