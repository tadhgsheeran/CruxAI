# Recovery

## What recovery is

Recovery is the physiological process by which the body restores itself between climbing efforts, whether between burns on a project, sessions in a week, or blocks in a training cycle. It includes muscular recovery (glycogen replenishment, protein synthesis, and inflammation resolution), connective tissue recovery (tendon and pulley adaptation, which lags behind muscle), and nervous system recovery (restoration of maximal force output). Sleep, nutrition, hydration, and load management are the primary variables. Recovery is not passive downtime, it is the period when adaptation actually occurs.

## When it matters

Recovery matters most on multi-day climbing trips, during projecting phases when repeated max-effort attempts stack fatigue, and in high-volume training blocks where accumulated load can tip into overtraining. It also matters between burns within a single session, where three to five minutes of active shaking and controlled breathing can restore enough capacity for another quality attempt. Ignoring recovery is one of the fastest routes to plateau or injury in climbing, particularly for climbers who train hard but never build in deload weeks.

## Common mistakes

Common mistakes include accumulating junk volume (moderate-intensity climbing that adds fatigue without adaptation), skipping deload weeks in a training cycle, and sleeping poorly during peak training periods. Nutritional undereating relative to training load is common, especially in climbers cutting weight. Hangboarding through finger fatigue, or trying to project through obvious tendon warning signs, are the highest-risk mistakes and the ones most likely to cause a lost season.

## Training and practice

Between-burn recovery uses shake outs (alternating hands overhead to promote forearm blood flow), hanging rests on jugs, and controlled nasal breathing to bring heart rate down. Between-session recovery is driven by sleep (aim for seven to nine hours), protein intake (roughly 1.6 to 2.2 grams per kilogram of bodyweight for training climbers), and easy movement on rest days. Between-block recovery uses deload weeks (reduce volume by 40 to 60 percent, maintain some intensity) roughly every four to six weeks. Tracking session RPE and morning resting heart rate reveals accumulated fatigue before it becomes injury.

## Safety

Overtraining shows up first as elevated resting heart rate, disrupted sleep, and stalled performance, then as tendon soreness that does not resolve overnight. A2 pulley strains and elbow tendinopathies are the most common recovery-failure injuries. If finger or elbow pain persists more than 48 hours after a session, reduce load immediately rather than pushing through, and consult a climbing-specific physiotherapist if it persists beyond a week.
