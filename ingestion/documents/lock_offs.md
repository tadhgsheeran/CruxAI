# Lock Offs

## What a lock off is

A lock off is a static upper-body position where you hold one arm bent, usually at or near ninety degrees, while the other arm reaches for the next hold. The pulling arm supports the majority of body weight in isometric contraction, keeping the shoulder engaged and the elbow near the ribs while the free arm moves precisely to a target. Unlike a dynamic move where momentum carries the body, a lock off relies on strength and control to keep the reach slow, deliberate, and repeatable. The position can be shallow (arm nearly straight), mid-range (around 90 degrees), or deep (hand near shoulder or chin).

## When it is useful

Lock offs matter when the next hold is small, poorly positioned for a catch, or requires exact placement of the fingers on a specific part of the grip. They are essential on slabs and vertical faces where dynamic moves would break friction, and on crux sections where a botched throw ends the attempt. In overhanging terrain, deep lock offs help you match small holds or clip from awkward positions. They are also key on onsight attempts, where committing to a dyno without knowing the hold is risky.

## Common mistakes

The most common mistake is chicken-winging the elbow, letting it drift away from the torso, which lengthens the lever and dramatically increases the force required. Climbers also let the hips swing off the wall, which shifts weight away from the feet and onto the pulling arm. Over-reliance on the bicep rather than the whole shoulder-and-back complex leads to premature fatigue, and starting the reach before fully locking the position causes the body to sag mid-move.

## Training and practice

Effective training includes weighted pull-up holds at various angles (top, 90 degrees, and 120 degrees) held for five to ten seconds. One-arm negatives, where you lower slowly from a two-arm pull-up to a one-arm hang, build the eccentric strength that lock offs demand. On the wall, deliberate slow reaches on moderate problems train control, and campus board lock-off work at sub-maximal levels builds contact strength without the injury risk of full campusing. Frenchies, which combine pull-ups with isometric holds at multiple positions, are a proven staple.

## Safety

Lock offs place high load on the elbow tendons and shoulder rotator cuff. Elbow tendinopathy (medial and lateral) is common in climbers who train them heavily without antagonist work. Balance lock-off training with pushing exercises like dips and shoulder presses, and avoid training deep lock offs when already fatigued.
